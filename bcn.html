Biopsychology, Cognition, & Neuroscience (BCN)

Where can BCN take me?

Choosing a major in Biopsychology, Cognition, and Neuroscience (BCN) can open the door to numerous career paths, each benefiting from a strong understanding of how the brain and mind work. This interdisciplinary major equips students with a comprehensive foundation in biology, psychology, and neuroscience, giving them the skills needed to thrive in a wide range of fields. Here’s what a BCN major can help students achieve:

  • Research Opportunities: A solid foundation in neuroscience and cognition prepares students to contribute to research projects in academic, clinical, or pharmaceutical settings. Understanding experimental design, data analysis, and laboratory techniques makes students competitive for research assistant roles and future graduate studies.
  • Healthcare and Medicine: Students interested in becoming doctors, therapists, or other healthcare professionals benefit from the major's emphasis on the biological basis of behavior and mental health. It provides a valuable understanding of how the brain impacts physical and psychological health, useful for careers like neurology, psychiatry, or neuropsychology.
  • Clinical and Counseling Roles: Those aiming for careers in psychology or mental health counseling can build a deep understanding of cognitive and emotional processes. This background is ideal for working in therapy, rehabilitation, or counseling settings where knowledge of brain-behavior relationships is essential.
  • Education and Advocacy: Students interested in science education or public health can leverage their understanding of biopsychology to teach and advocate for mental health awareness, neuroscience literacy, and wellness education.
  • Graduate Studies: With a BCN major, students are well-prepared for pursuing graduate degrees in fields like neuroscience, psychology, public health, or even law, particularly in areas that intersect with cognitive science and mental health.

Resume Breakdown

For students majoring in Biopsychology, Cognition, and Neuroscience (BCN), a well-crafted resume should highlight academic achievements, research experience, relevant skills, and extracurricular activities that showcase their commitment to the field. Here's a breakdown of key elements to include:

  1. Contact Information
    • Full name, phone number, professional email address, and LinkedIn profile (if applicable).
    • Optional: Personal website or portfolio if you have one, especially if you have research or projects to display.
  2. Education
    • Degree: Bachelor of Science in Biopsychology, Cognition, and Neuroscience (include expected graduation date).
    • Relevant coursework: List classes like Behavioral Neuroscience, Cognitive Psychology, Neuroanatomy, Research Methods, and Statistical Analysis.
    • Honors or awards: Academic distinctions, scholarships, or Dean’s List recognition.
  3. Research Experience
    • Lab or Research Assistant Positions: Describe research projects, the techniques you used (e.g., electrophysiology, neuroimaging, behavioral analysis), and the outcomes or skills gained.
    • Independent Research Projects: Mention any senior thesis, independent study projects, or research papers, detailing the hypothesis, methods, and findings.
    • Presentations and Publications: If you presented at a conference or published work, include the titles and venues.
  4. Relevant Skills
    • Technical Skills: List lab techniques (e.g., EEG, fMRI analysis, animal handling, microscopy), programming languages (like Python, R, or MATLAB for data analysis), and software proficiency (e.g., SPSS, Excel, or statistical software).
    • Research and Data Analysis: Emphasize skills in experimental design, statistical analysis, and data interpretation.
    • Soft Skills: Highlight teamwork, communication, problem-solving, and critical thinking skills developed through collaborative projects or research work.
  5. Clinical or Healthcare Experience (if applicable)
    • Internships or volunteer work in hospitals, clinics, or mental health settings.
    • Shadowing experiences with doctors, psychologists, or neuropsychologists, detailing the skills or insights gained.
  6. Extracurricular Activities and Leadership
    • Relevant Clubs: Membership in neuroscience or psychology societies, roles in student government, or participation in academic journals or societies.
    • Community Involvement: Volunteering for mental health awareness initiatives, tutoring science subjects, or participating in outreach programs.
    • Leadership Roles: Experience leading clubs or organizations, organizing events, or mentoring other students.
  7. Professional Experience (if applicable)
    • Jobs or internships, even if unrelated to neuroscience, can demonstrate valuable skills like time management, teamwork, and customer service.
    • Science Communication: Experience writing about neuroscience for blogs, creating educational content, or participating in public science events.
  8. Certifications and Trainings
    • Any additional coursework, certifications, or workshops related to neuroscience or research methods (e.g., CITI training for research ethics).
  9. Languages
    • Proficiency in additional languages, especially if relevant to your future career goals or research interests.

Interviews for Internships or Lab Opportunities

  • Know your background and be ready to explain it
    • Highlight relevant classwork and research experience
  • Do research about labs and be prepared to talk about research interests
  • Prepare for common interview questions
    • Why are you interested in this position?
    • What are you strengths and weaknesses?
    • Describe a time you overcome a challenge in a research or academic setting.
  • Ask insightful questions
    • What is the lab’s approach to training new research assistants
    • How do you see this project evolving over the next year, and how might I contribute?
    • What skills or qualities do you think are most important for success in this internship?
  • Emphasize eagerness to learn
  • Show a collaborative mindset, as research involves working on a team
  • Follow up with a thank you note
